About

How Finding The Finger works

The finger pointing at the moon is not the moon.

— Zen proverb

Finding The Finger is a semantic similarity search engine across sacred and philosophical texts from traditions including Abrahamic, Buddhist, Dharmic, Confucian, Norse, Shinto, Sikh, Taoist, and Zoroastrian corpora. Different traditions often point at the same things — this tool tries to find where those fingers align.

⚙️ How it works

Every passage in the dataset is converted into a dense vector embedding — a list of numbers that captures its semantic meaning. Passages with similar meaning end up close together in this high-dimensional space, regardless of language, tradition, or literal wording.

A passage is the smallest semantically coherent unit we could identify for each tradition — the atom of meaning that the tradition itself tends to reference and quote. This varies: a verse in the Bible, a shloka in the Bhagavad Gita, a tuk in the Sri Guru Granth Sahib, and so on. Where a tradition has no clear sub-unit, the smallest consistently-cited division was used.

Searches work by embedding your query (or the selected passage) into the same space, then finding the nearest neighbours by cosine similarity.

The map view uses UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection) to reduce the embeddings to two dimensions so you can visually explore how traditions cluster and overlap.

🧠 Embedding model

Embeddings are generated using nomic-embed-text-v1.5, a state-of-the-art open-source text embedding model by Nomic AI. It produces 768-dimensional vectors optimised for semantic similarity tasks. All embeddings are stored in PostgreSQL with the pgvector extension, enabling fast nearest-neighbour search at scale.

📚 Higher-level passages

The corpus is structured hierarchically — verses sit inside chapters, which sit inside books. Raw embeddings exist only at the verse level.

For chapter- or book-level searches, embeddings are aggregated on the fly: all descendant verse embeddings are collected, averaged uniformly, and re-normalised onto the unit hypersphere. This treats every verse as an equal contributor and gives a centroid that represents the passage as a whole.

Aggregation is inherently lossy. Averaging embeddings compresses a chapter's worth of distinct ideas into a single point — thematic diversity, narrative arc, and tension between verses are all flattened out. The resulting vector is a rough centre of gravity, not a true semantic representation of the whole. Re-embedding at the chapter or book level using a model with a longer context window would produce meaningfully better results; this is a known limitation and a likely area for future improvement.

⚠️ Disclaimers

Similarity is semantic, not theological. A high similarity score means the passages use comparable concepts, imagery, or language — it does not imply equivalence of meaning, doctrinal agreement, or any claim about the relationship between traditions.

Translations matter. All texts are in English translation. Similarity scores reflect the translated text, not the original — different translations of the same passage may score differently.

This is a tool for exploration, not scholarship. Results should be treated as starting points for further reading, not as authoritative comparative analysis.
